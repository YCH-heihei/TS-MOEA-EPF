\begin{abstract}
Multiobjective optimization algorithms based on estimated Pareto front (PF) have achieved promising results. However, the complexity of irregular PFs and insufficient convergence of Pareto nondominated solutions can lead to significant errors in estimating the PF, misguiding the selection of solutions. To address this, we propose a two-stage multiobjective optimization algorithm based on estimated Pareto front (TS-MOEA-EPF). In the first stage, an improved growing neural gas (GNG) guides the population to maintain diversity while converging steadily, thus providing Pareto nondominated solutions close to the true PF. In the second stage, a neural network is used to estimate the PF more flexibly. We {then} use the projection points of solutions on the estimated PF to more directly and effectively measure the convergence and diversity of  solutions, thereby  iteratively selecting  solutions with good convergence and diversity for the next population one by one. To verify the effectiveness of TS-MOEA-EPF, we conducted comparative experiments with {thirteen} advanced algorithms on 105 {benchmark} instances and five practical problems. The results show that TS-MOEA-EPF performs well on most  problems, particularly those with irregular PFs or when  solutions are far from the true PFs early in the optimization.
\end{abstract}

{The implementation and datasets of MOKP  and MOTSP are provided by PlatEMO \cite{PlatEMO}. When calculating the HV \cite{HV}, the nadir points used  are: $(43, 4.2, 12.5)$ for CSI, (1.7046e+3, 11, 0.3) for CWDV, (8.3061e+4, 1350, 2.8535e+6, 1.5965e+7, 3.4791e+5) for WRP. The ideal points for all  problems are set to the origin. For handling constraints, a penalty term is added to each objective, equal to the number of violated constraints multiplied by the maximum value of that objective. This ensuring that feasible solutions dominate those with constraint violations.}	
\par {To better present the  results, the data of TS-MOE-EPF and the 13 comparative algorithms are split into two separate tables. The HV values obtained by each algorithm on these problems are listed in Table \ref{table:the average HV of MaOPs on Pratical Problems1} and Table \ref{table:the average HV of MaOPs on Pratical Problems2}, respectively.  TS-MOEA-EPF achieved three best values on MOKP and WRP, while HEA and RVEA-iGNG outperformed TS-MOEA-EPF on  MOTSP. Specifically, HEA achieved two best values on  MOSTP with 3, 10 objectives, and RVEA-iGNG achieved one best value on the 15-objective MOTSP. CA-MOEA performed best on CWDV, while AdaW and AR-MOEA performed best on 3-objective MOKP and CSI, respectively.} 
\par {Fig. \ref{fig:the result on pratical problems} shows the distribution of nondominated solutions obtained by  representative  algorithms for the above problems. From Fig. \ref{fig:the result on pratical problems}, it can be  observed that TS-MOEA-EPF can maintain good diversity on  3-objective CWDV, CSI and MOKP. HEA and MultiGPO show room for improvement in terms of uniformity on the 5-objective WRP.  On MOTSP and MOKP, the distribution of solutions obtained by each algorithm appear to be irregular, which also reflects the complexity of PFs in practical problems. Compared with HEA and CARV-MOEA,  RVEA-iGNG and TS-MOEA-EPF  cover more objective space on 15-objective MOKP, especially for the 12-th to 15-th objectives.}

To investigate whether their performance on IGD+ and HV  is impacted by insufficient convergence or uneven distribution, we evaluate additional metrics: Riesz s-energy $E_s$ \cite{RieszSEnergy} for population diversity, GD+ \cite{IGDp} for convergence, and Spacing \cite{Spacing} for uniformity.  The calculation of $E_{s}$  follows Eq. (\ref{eq:E_s}), where  $s$ is set to 2. For all three metrics, smaller values indicate better performance. Furthermore, we use  Eq. (\ref{Eq:boundaryCoverage}) {to measure} how far the population is from the center and how close it is to the boundary (Boundary Coverage). The results of each indicator are shown in  Table \ref{Table:more metrics on 15-IDTLZ1}. 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:E_s}
	E_s(\mathcal{A})=\sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_{j=1,j\neq i}^{|\mathcal{A}|}k_s(\vec{a}_i,\vec{a}_j)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
	k_s(\vec{a}_i,\vec{a}_j) = 
	\begin{cases}
		\frac{1}{||\vec{a}_i-\vec{a}_j||^s}, &\vec{a}_i  \neq \vec{a}_j \\
		0, & \text{otherwise}
	\end{cases}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{Eq:boundaryCoverage}
	BC(P)=\underset{\boldsymbol{p}\in P}{\sum}\frac{{\angle(\boldsymbol{p},(1,1,\ldots,1))}}{|P|}
\end{equation}

	\section{Analysis of Neural Network and GNG}\label{Sec:NeuralAndGNG}                              across PFs 
	\par {In this section, we analyze the performance of the neural network and  GNG   in TS-MOEA-EPF across  PFs with varying shapes,  $M$, and  $N$. We use WFG7 (regular), MaF1 and MaF4 (inverted), MaF3 (highly convex), MaF6  (degenerated), MaF7 (disconnected), MaF2 and MaF8 (partial), and MaF9   as representative test problems. It is worth mentioning that the manifold of a degenerated PF has fewer than $M-1$ dimensions. Thus,  as $M$ increases, the proportion of degenerated PFs in the objective space  sharply decreases and becomes increasingly sparse.}
	\subsection{{Analysis of Neural Network}}
	\par To measure the fitting ability of the neural network for PFs with varying shapes, $N$ and  $M$, we first  normalize the  10,000 uniformly sampled points {$opt$} on the true PF using $\boldsymbol{z}^{min}$ and $\boldsymbol{z}^{max}$, obtaining $opt^{\prime}$. Here,  $\boldsymbol{z}^{min}$ and $\boldsymbol{z}^{max}$ refer to the ideal point and nadir point used to normalize the training set for neural network, which are obtained at  90$\%$ {of the}  optimization progress. We then use Eq. (\ref{eq:pp_x})  to compute the corresponding projection points, where $output(\boldsymbol{x})$ denotes the network's output for input $\boldsymbol{x}$. These projection points are restored to the original objective space (before normalization) using Eq. (\ref{Eq:pp_prime}),  followed by re-normalization with the true PF’s ideal point $\boldsymbol{z}^{\ast,min}$ and nadir point $\boldsymbol{z}^{\ast,max}$. Finally, we use Eq. (\ref{Eq:MAS_N}) to measure the fitting ability of the neural network. Intuitively, the smaller the $MAE_N$ value, the closer the actual sampling points are to their projected points on the estimated PF. 
	% and use Eq. (\ref{Eq:STD_N}) to evaluate the shape similarity between the estimated PFs and the true PFs.
	\par {Meanwhile, to investigate the impact of the number of nondominated solutions on the neural network's ability to approximate the true PF, we executed TS-MOEA-EPF with different population sizes: $0.5N$, $N$, $2N$, and $3N$.  Specifically, for $M=3$: $0.5N=55$, $N=105$, $2N=210$, $3N=300$; for $M=10$: $0.5N=110$, $N=275$, $2N=440$, $3N=770$; for $M=15$: $0.5N=30$, $N=135$, $2N=240$, $3N=800$. We ensured that $FE_{max}$ remained consistent across different population sizes, adhering to the setting of $M\times 10,000$. The corresponding results are presented  in Table \ref{Table: MAE about Neural network}. In this table, the value in the last column  refers to the mean  and variance of the objective sums for $opt^\ast$.} 
	\begin{align}
		{\boldsymbol{pp}}(\boldsymbol{opt}_i^\prime)=\frac{\boldsymbol{opt}_i^\prime}{\sum_{j=1}^{M}opt_{i,j}}\cdot output(\frac{\boldsymbol{opt}_i^\prime}{\sum_{j=1}^{M}opt_{i,j}})\label{eq:pp_x}\\
		pp_{i,j}^{\ast}=\frac{{{pp}_j}(\boldsymbol{opt}_i^\prime)\times(z_j^{max}-z_j^{min})+z_j^{min}-z_j^{\ast,min}}{(z_j^{\ast,max}-z_j^{\ast,min})}\label{Eq:pp_prime}\\
		opt^\ast_{i,j}=\frac{opt_{i,j}-z_j^{\ast,min}}{(z_j^{\ast,max}-z_j^{\ast,min})}\label{Eq:opt_prime}\\
		MAE_N=\frac{1}{|opt^{\ast}|}\sum_{i=1}^{|opt^\ast|}|\frac{\sum_{j=1}^{M}pp_{i,j}^{\ast}}{\sum_{j=1}^{M}opt^{\ast}_{i,j}}-1|\label{Eq:MAS_N}
	\end{align}
	
	% The smaller the $STD_N$, the more similar the differences between any sampling point and its corresponding projection point. When $STD_N$ is 0, the estimated PF is a translation of the true PF.
	%\begin{equation}\label{Eq:STD_N}
	%	STD_N=\sqrt{\frac{1}{|opt^{*}|}\sum_{i=1}^{|opt^*|}(|\frac{\sum_{j=1}^{M}pp_{i,j}^{*}}{\sum_{j=1}^{M}opt^{*}_{i,j}}-1|-MAE_N)^2}
	%\end{equation}
	
	\par{As shown in Table \ref{Table: MAE about Neural network}, $MAE_N$ is less than $1e-1$ for all problems, except  MaF3 and MaF6 with 10 and 15 objectives, as well as WFG7 and MaF2 with 15 objectives. This suggests the neural network can  effectively approximate most PFs. The $MAE_N$  of MaF3 and MaF6 are relatively less ideal compared to other problems. The PF of MaF3 is highly convex: as $M$ increases, the objective sums near its center approach 0, while those near the boundary approach 1. This significant discrepancy in output values easily cause overfitting. For MaF6, the true PF occupies a small portion of the objective space with highly similar input directions but large output differences (large variance of $\sum_{j=1}^{M}{opt^\ast_{\sim,j}}$), also leading to overfitting.} {On the other hand, $MAE_N$ is  good for problem  with small output differences, such as MaF1 and MaF9, respectively. Therefore, appropriately increasing input diversity while reducing output discrepancy may significantly aid neural network in approximating the true PF.} 
	\par {Regarding the influence of $N$, except for MaF1, MaF3, and MaF6, $MAE_N$  gradually decreases from $0.5N$ to $2N$ but rises again at $3N$. This may occur because the neural network learns too many noisy samples at $3N$ and suffers from overfitting, or because $3N$ is too large, leading to slow convergence such that the population cannot sufficiently approach the true PF. Based on the Friedman's test \cite{Friedman} at the 0.05 significance level  for $MAE_N$, a population size of $2N$ yields the best approximation to the true PF.}

	\subsection{{Analysis of GNG}}
	%\par We use $N_{GNG}$ and $N_{nb}$ to record the final number of nodes in the GNG and the average number of neighbors per node when the update progress stops, respectively. Ideally, $N_{GNG}$ should match $N$, otherwise, it indicates that the training data distribution of GNG fluctuates too much, making it difficult for GNG to learn an approximate distribution. $N_ {nb}$ should increase with $M$ and $N$, but it must remain within a reasonable range. If $N_{nb}$ is too small, it suggests that the data distribution information collected by GNG is insufficient, and each node is prone to become an isolated node and be deleted. Conversely, if $N_{nb}$ is too large, it may cause the nodes to easily approach the center. 
	\par {In this section, we analyze and compare the differences in the PF approximation ability of the GNG networks employed by TS-MOEA-EPF and DEA-GNG. We use the average minimum distance between GNG nodes, denoted as $avg(dis)$, to measure the diversity of GNG nodes. Meanwhile, the variance of these minimum distances, denoted as $var(dis)$, is employed to assess the uniformity of GNG nodes. A larger  $avg(dis)$  indicates better  diversity, while a smaller $var(dis)$  indicates a more uniform  distribution. }
	\par {To measure whether GNG can effectively approximate discontinuous PFs, we introduce $\theta_{G}$ as shown in Eq. (\ref{Eq:theta_valid}), where $NS$ is the GNG nodes. When a large number of GNG nodes are distributed in discontinuous regions and their objective vectors do not intersect with the true PF,  $\theta_{G}$ will be very large. Additionally, we use $IGD_G$ as shown in the Eq. (\ref{Eq:IGD_valid}) to more intuitively determine whether GNG nodes can approximate the true PF. The smaller the $IGD_{G}$, the better the node distribution approximate the true PF.}
	
	\begin{align}
		NS^{\prime}_{i,j}=NS_{i,j}\times({z^{max}_j-z^{min}_j})+z^{min}_j\\
		NS^{\ast}_{i,j}=\frac{(NS^\prime_{i,j}-z^{\ast,min}_j)}{z^{\ast,max}_j-z^{\ast,min}_j}\\
		\theta_{G}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{|opt^\ast|}\underset{\boldsymbol{r}\in NS^{\ast}}{\min}{\angle(\boldsymbol{opt}^\ast_i,\boldsymbol{r})}}{|opt^\ast|}\label{Eq:theta_valid}\\
		IGD_{G}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{|opt|}\underset{\boldsymbol{r}\in NS^\prime}{\min}{||\boldsymbol{opt}_i-\boldsymbol{r}||_2}}{|opt|}\label{Eq:IGD_valid}
	\end{align}
	
	\par {The test problems include   regular (WFG7, MaF1), inverted (MaF4), degenerated (MaF6, MaF4), highly convex (MaF3), disconnected (MaF7), partial (MaF2 and MaF8) ones, with 3, 10 and 15 objective.} Regarding $avg(dis)$, {the above  metrics of GNG used by DEA-GNG and TS-MOEA-EPF are shown in  Table \ref{Table:MAE about GNG network} (superscript T: TS-MOEA-EPF, superscript D: DEA-GNG).  TS-MOEA-EPF shows better node diversity than DEA-GNG, except for MaF3, MaF4, and MaF9. The reason why DEA-GNG exhibits better node diversity on these problems may be attributed to the fact that it also learns and retains outlier nodes that are far from the true PF while still preserving high diversity.  However, the $IGD_G$ of DEA-GNG on MaF3, MaF4, and MaF9 is inferior to that of TS-MOEA-EPF, indicating that the nodes of DEA-GNG does not approximate the true PF well.}

	\subsection{Analysis of $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$}
	\par $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ jointly control the sensitivity of the potential outlier detection in  Algorithm 3.	$\theta_2$ serves as the base threshold, while $\theta_1$  adjusts the detection strictness according to angular differences. Smaller values of $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ result in more  lenient detection, making solutions more likely to be classified as outliers. Conversely, larger values lead to stricter  outlier identification. 	
	\par  To analyze the impact of $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ in {Algorithm 3}, we  set $c_{div}$ and $c_{cov}$ to 0.60 and 0.80, respectively, while disabling  {Algorithm 4}.  The selected test problems cover various PFs, including the highly convex VNT2; the 10-objective MaF9, DTLZ3,  and MaF3, which are prone to  encounter outliers; the discontinuous MaF7 and WFG2; the inverted MaF4; the degenerate DTLZ6; and the regular MaF5. These problems involve 3, 10, and 15 objectives, ensuring a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm’s performance across different scenarios. To determine appropriate values for $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$,  they are gradually increased from 2.0 and 1.2 to 6.0 and 3.0, respectively. During this process, their values are further fine-tuned for local optimization. For example, $\theta_1$ is adjusted from 3 to 3.5, and $\theta_2$ from 2 to 1.5.
	\par  The  IGD+ results in Table \ref{table:experiment on theta1 and theta2}  indicate that $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ significantly affect performance  on problems such as MaF3, MaF4,  DTLZ3 and VNT2. For MaF3, MaF4 and DTLZ3, extreme points are often selected from outliers with poor convergence,   Algorithm {3} can detect such solutions and avoid misclassifying them as extreme points. In contrast, for VNT2 with highly convex PF,   small $\theta_1$ and  small $\theta_2$ value (such as $\theta_1=2$ and $\theta_2=1.5$)  may incorrectly classify true {Pareto nondominated} solutions as outliers. {Therefore, $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ should not be too small.} When $\theta_1\geq3.5$ and $\theta_2\geq1.5$, Algorithm {3}  exhibits no adverse effects on VNT2. Meanwhile, the above parameter settings have little  impact on problems with regular PFs {(e.g., MaF5)}.  
	\par Based on the Friedman's test  at the 0.05 level and the average ranking shown in Table \ref{table:experiment on theta1 and theta2}, we set $\theta_1$ to $3.5$ and $\theta_2$ to $1.5$,  {respectively}.

	\subsection{Analysis of $c_{cov}$  and  $c_{div}$.}
    \par This section primarily evaluates the performance of $c_{cov}$ and $c_{div}$ in balancing diversity and convergence across various PFs. $c_{cov}$ and $c_{div}$  respectively determine the weights of convergence and diversity in Algorithm 9. Larger $c_{cov}$ reduces the scaled differences in convergence among solutions and lowers its weight in fitness evaluation; similarly, larger $c_{div}$ reduces the scaled differences in diversity and decreases its corresponding weight. Thus, the algorithm prioritizes convergence when $c_{cov}$ is small and $c_{div}$ is large, and favors diversity otherwise. In the parameter settings,  we start with $c_{cov}=0.6$, ${c_{div}=0.9}$ and  gradually adjust to $c_{cov}=0.9, {c_{div}=0.6}$. The former parameter setting favors convergence, while the latter setting emphasizes diversity.
	\par  For test problems, we selected test problems that are less prone to encounter outliers, including regular WFG7, MaF2 and MaF8 with partial PFs, discontinuous MaF7 and MaF11, degenerated IMOP4 and DTLZ6, with $M$ ranging from 3 to 15. For the sensitivity analysis of  $c_{cov}$ and  $c_{div}$, we set  $\theta_1$ to 3.5 and $\theta_2$ to $1.5$,  respectively, disabling Algorithm {4}. The IGD+ results in Table \ref{table:experiment on cCov and cDiv} show that {TS-MOEA-EPF} is not highly sensitive to $c_{cov}$ and  $c_{div}$.   Diversity should be emphasized more than convergence for MaF7 and MaF8. Because smaller  $c_{div}$ ($c_{cov}$) tends to amplify differences in solution diversity (convergence), and on MaF7 and MaF8 the performance with $c_{cov}=0.6$ and $c_{div}=0.9$  is inferior to that with $c_{cov}=0.9$ and $c_{div}=0.6$. 
	\par Based on the Friedman's test at the 0.05 level and the average rankings, we set {$c_{cov}$} and {$c_{div}$} to 0.70 and 0.60, respectively.
